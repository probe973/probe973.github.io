Descriptive Statistics in R Studio

A Guide to Understanding and Summarizing Data using R

This guide will walk you through the process of obtaining summary statistics and creating relevant graphs for different types of variables using R Studio. Understanding descriptive statistics is a foundational skill in data analysis, providing essential insights into the characteristics of your dataset.

Descriptive statistics help us understand the basic features of the data in a study. They provide simple summaries about the sample and the measures, forming the basis of virtually every quantitative analysis.

Before you begin, ensure you have your omni.csv file available. Throughout this guide, we will use specific variables from your dataset to illustrate the concepts. You will also be prompted to apply the steps to other relevant variables:

  • final_exam_score: Represents the numerical score achieved by students on a final exam.
  • study_hours_per_week: Indicates the number of hours a student reported studying each week.
  • preferred_study_method: Describes a student's chosen study strategy (e.g., 'Flashcards', 'Summarizing', 'Practice Tests').
  • study_session_group: A categorical variable indicating the type of study session group a student was assigned to (e.g., 'High', 'Medium', 'Low').
  • used_study_support_services: A categorical variable indicating whether a student utilized study support services (Yes/No). This variable will be used to demonstrate splitting analyses by groups.

To follow along, first load your data into R. Assuming omni.csv is in your working directory:


data <- read.csv("omni.csv")

The `read.csv()` function is used to load data from a CSV (Comma Separated Values) file into a data frame in R. In this command, `"omni.csv"` is the path to your file. The loaded data will be stored in an object named `data`. 


head(data)

The `head()` function displays the first six rows of a data frame (or other R objects) by default. This is useful for quickly checking the structure and content of your loaded data.

Part 1: Analyzing Interval/Ratio Variables

Interval/ratio variables are numerical variables where the differences between values are meaningful. For example, a 10-point difference in exam scores means the same regardless of the score range. Ratio variables also have a true zero point (e.g., age, income, test scores, study hours), where zero indicates an absence of the quantity. For these types of variables, we typically examine measures of central tendency (mean, median, mode) and dispersion (standard deviation, variance, range).

1.2 Interpreting Summary Statistics for Interval/Ratio Variables

Here's what each statistic tells us:

  • N & Missing: R's `summary()` function and other functions often remove `NA` (Not Available) values by default or with `na.rm = TRUE`. You can check missing values with `sum(is.na(data$variable_name))`.
  • Mean: The arithmetic average of all values. It is calculated by summing all values and dividing by the number of observations. The mean is sensitive to extreme values (outliers), meaning it can be pulled towards very high or very low scores.
  • Median: The middle value when data is ordered from smallest to largest. If there's an even number of observations, it's the average of the two middle values. The median is less affected by outliers than the mean, making it a good measure of typical value in skewed distributions.
  • Mode: The value that appears most frequently in the dataset. A dataset can have one mode, multiple modes (bimodal or multimodal), or no mode if all values occur with the same frequency. R does not have a built-in `mode()` function for numerical data in the statistical sense (it refers to storage mode). You'd typically infer this from a histogram or write a custom function.
  • Standard Deviation (`sd()`): A measure of the typical distance data points are from the mean. A smaller standard deviation indicates data points are clustered closely around the mean, while a larger one indicates more spread or variability in the data.
  • Variance (`var()`): The average of the squared differences from the Mean. It is the square of the standard deviation. It gives a numerical value describing the spread of data points.
  • Minimum (`min()`) & Maximum (`max()`): The smallest and largest values observed in your dataset for that variable. This gives you the range of your data.
  • Skewness (`skew()` from `psych` package): Indicates the symmetry of the distribution.
    • If **Skewness is close to 0**, the distribution is relatively symmetrical (e.g., a bell curve). In symmetrical distributions, the mean, median, and mode are often very close to each other.
    • If **Skewness is positive (>0)**, the tail of the distribution extends to the right (positive direction). This typically means there are more lower values, with a few higher, extreme values pulling the mean to the right of the median. In this case, the Mean > Median.
    • If **Skewness is negative (<0)**, the tail extends to the left (negative direction). This typically means there are more higher values, with a few lower, extreme values pulling the mean to the left of the median. In this case, the Mean < Median.
  • Kurtosis (`kurtosi()` from `psych` package): Indicates the "tailedness" or peakedness of the distribution compared to a normal distribution.
    • If **Kurtosis is positive (>0)** (leptokurtic), it suggests heavier tails and a more peaked distribution, potentially indicating more outliers (extreme values).
    • If **Kurtosis is negative (<0)** (platykurtic), it suggests lighter tails and a flatter distribution, with fewer extreme values than a normal distribution.
    • If **Kurtosis is close to 0** (mesokurtic), it resembles the tailedness and peakedness of a normal distribution.

1.3 Creating Graphs for Interval/Ratio Variables (Histograms & Box Plots)

Graphs provide powerful visual insights into the distribution of your data, complementing the numerical summaries. In R, plots will appear in the "Plots" pane of R Studio.

  1. Use `hist()` for histograms and `boxplot()` for box plots.
    2. 
hist(data$final_exam_score,
     main = "Histogram of Final Exam Scores",
     xlab = "Final Exam Score",
     ylab = "Frequency",
     col = "steelblue",
     border = "white")

    The `hist()` function creates a histogram.

    • The first argument, `data$final_exam_score`, specifies the numerical variable for which to create the histogram.
    • `main` sets the title of the plot.
    • `xlab` sets the label for the x-axis.
    • `ylab` sets the label for the y-axis.
    • `col` sets the fill color of the bars.
    • `border` sets the color of the borders around the bars.

    
boxplot(data$final_exam_score,
        main = "Box Plot of Final Exam Scores",
        ylab = "Final Exam Score",
        col = "lightgreen",
        border = "darkgreen")

    The `boxplot()` function creates a box plot.

    • The first argument, `data$final_exam_score`, specifies the numerical variable for which to create the box plot.
    • `main` sets the title of the plot.
    • `ylab` sets the label for the y-axis.
    • `col` sets the fill color of the box.
    • `border` sets the color of the borders of the box and whiskers.

    Running these commands will generate the plots in your R Studio "Plots" pane. Observe the shapes and features of these plots.

1.4 Interpreting Graphs for Interval/Ratio Variables

  • Histogram: Displays the shape of the distribution, showing where values are concentrated, identifying peaks (modes), gaps, and overall symmetry or skewness. Each bar represents a range of values (or "bin"), and its height shows the frequency of scores within that range.
  • Box Plot: Provides a clear visual representation of the "five-number summary": minimum, first quartile (Q1), median (Q2), third quartile (Q3), and maximum. It is particularly effective for identifying outliers.
    • The box itself represents the **Interquartile Range (IQR)**, which contains the middle 50% of your data (between Q1 and Q3).
    • The line inside the box is the **median**.
    • The "whiskers" typically extend to the most extreme data points within 1.5 times the IQR from the box.
    • Individual points (often shown as dots or asterisks) beyond the whiskers are considered **outliers**, meaning they are unusually far from the rest of the data.

Part 2: Analyzing Categorical Variables

Categorical variables represent types of data which can be divided into groups or categories. This includes nominal variables (categories without inherent order, e.g., 'preferred study method') and ordinal variables (categories with a meaningful order, e.g., satisfaction ratings like 'Low', 'Medium', 'High'). For categorical variables, we focus on frequencies and proportions rather than means and standard deviations, as these mathematical operations are not meaningful for non-numerical categories.

2.1 Obtaining Frequency Tables for Categorical Variables (e.g., preferred_study_method)

  1. Load your omni.csv file into an R data frame.
  2. To get frequency counts and percentages for a categorical variable, use the `table()` and `prop.table()` functions.
    3. 
data$preferred_study_method <- as.factor(data$preferred_study_method)

    The `as.factor()` function converts a variable into a factor (R's term for a categorical variable). This is crucial for R to correctly interpret and handle categorical data in many functions, ensuring it doesn't treat categories as numbers. 

    
counts <- table(data$preferred_study_method)
print(counts)

    The `table()` function generates a frequency table, showing the count of observations for each unique value (category) of the specified variable. 

    
percentages <- prop.table(counts) * 100
print(percentages)

    The `prop.table()` function converts a frequency table (like `counts`) into a table of proportions. Multiplying the result by `100` converts these proportions into percentages, making them easier to interpret.

    Example Output:

    
# Counts
Flashcards Practice Tests   Summarizing
        36            31            33

# Percentages
Flashcards Practice Tests   Summarizing
      36.0          31.0          33.0

2.2 Interpreting Frequency Tables for Categorical Variables

A frequency table lists each category of your variable and shows how many times it appears in your data (Count or Frequency) and its proportion relative to the total (Percent or Percentage).

Preferred Study Method Count % of Total
Flashcards 36 36.0%
Summarizing 33 33.0%
Practice Tests 31 31.0%
  • Count: The number of participants who selected or fall into each specific category.
  • %: The percentage of participants in each category out of the total valid cases.

2.3 Creating Graphs for Categorical Variables (Bar Charts & Pie Charts)

Bar charts and pie charts are effective ways to visually represent the distribution of categorical variables. Remember to run the `table()` function first to get the counts.

  1. Use `barplot()` for bar charts and `pie()` for pie charts.
    2. 
barplot(counts,
        main = "Bar Chart of Preferred Study Methods",
        xlab = "Study Method",
        ylab = "Count",
        col = c("lightblue", "lightcoral", "lightgreen"),
        border = "darkblue")

    The `barplot()` function creates a bar chart.

    • The first argument, `counts`, is the table of frequencies you created.
    • `main` sets the title of the plot.
    • `xlab` sets the label for the x-axis (categories).
    • `ylab` sets the label for the y-axis (counts).
    • `col` sets the fill colors for each bar. You can provide a vector of colors, one for each bar.
    • `border` sets the color of the borders around the bars.

    
pie(counts,
    main = "Pie Chart of Preferred Study Methods",
    col = c("lightblue", "lightcoral", "lightgreen"),
    labels = paste0(names(counts), " (", percentages, "%)"))

    The `pie()` function creates a pie chart.

    • The first argument, `counts`, is the table of frequencies.
    • `main` sets the title of the plot.
    • `col` sets the fill colors for each slice.
    • `labels` provides the text labels for each slice. The `paste0()` function concatenates strings without a separator, and `names(counts)` gets the names of the categories from your `counts` table. This creates labels like "Flashcards (36.0%)".

    Running these commands will generate the plots in your R Studio "Plots" pane. Observe the visual distribution of categories.

2.4 Interpreting Graphs for Categorical Variables

  • Bar Chart: Each bar represents a distinct category, and its height (or length) corresponds to the frequency or percentage of observations in that category. Bar charts are generally preferred over pie charts for clarity and ease of comparison, especially when comparing frequencies across multiple categories or when categories have similar proportions.
  • Pie Chart: The entire circle represents 100% of the data, and each slice represents a category's proportion of the whole. While visually intuitive for showing parts of a whole, it can be challenging to accurately compare the sizes of slices when categories have similar proportions or when there are many categories.

Part 3: Descriptive Statistics by Groups (Splitting Data by Factors)

In data analysis, it is often insightful to understand if a numerical variable's distribution differs across distinct categories of another variable. This is known as "splitting" or "grouping" your data. For example, we might want to see if the final exam scores vary between students who used study support services and those who did not.

3.1 Analyzing Interval/Ratio Variable by a Categorical Factor (e.g., final exam scores by used_study_support_services)

  1. Load your omni.csv file into an R data frame.
  2. To calculate descriptive statistics for a numerical variable grouped by a categorical variable, you can use the `aggregate()` function.
    3. 
data$used_study_support_services <- as.factor(data$used_study_support_services)

    Similar to the previous section, this line ensures that the `used_study_support_services` column is treated as a categorical factor by R, which is necessary for grouping. 

    
aggregate(final_exam_score ~ used_study_support_services, data = data, FUN = mean, na.rm = TRUE)

    The `aggregate()` function applies a function (`FUN`) to a numerical variable, grouped by categories of another variable.

    • `final_exam_score ~ used_study_support_services` is a formula. In R formulas, `~` means "explained by" or "grouped by." So, this reads as "final exam score grouped by used study support services."
    • `data = data` specifies the data frame where these variables are located.
    • `FUN = mean` specifies that the `mean()` function should be applied to each group. You can replace `mean` with `median`, `sd`, `min`, `max`, or any other function.
    • `na.rm = TRUE` handles missing values within each group's calculation.

    You would repeat the `aggregate()` command, changing `FUN` to `median` and `sd` for those respective statistics.

    
aggregate(final_exam_score ~ used_study_support_services, data = data, FUN = median, na.rm = TRUE)
aggregate(final_exam_score ~ used_study_support_services, data = data, FUN = sd, na.rm = TRUE)

    Example Output (for `aggregate` means):

    
  used_study_support_services final_exam_score
1                          No         72.33333
2                         Yes         76.90385
  3. To create grouped box plots, `boxplot()` can directly handle this with a formula.
    4. 
boxplot(final_exam_score ~ used_study_support_services, data = data,
        main = "Final Exam Scores by Study Support Service Usage",
        xlab = "Used Study Support Services",
        ylab = "Final Exam Score",
        col = c("lightpink", "lightcyan"),
        border = "darkgray")

    This `boxplot()` command uses a formula syntax similar to `aggregate()`.

    • `final_exam_score ~ used_study_support_services` tells `boxplot()` to create separate box plots of `final_exam_score` for each category in `used_study_support_services`.
    • `data = data` specifies the data frame.
    • `main`, `xlab`, `ylab`, `col`, and `border` arguments are used for customizing the plot's appearance, as explained in Section 1.3.

    Running this command will generate the grouped box plot in your R Studio "Plots" pane.

3.2 Interpreting Grouped Descriptive Statistics

By comparing the statistics and plots for each group, you can identify differences in the central tendency, spread, and shape of the numerical variable's distribution across your defined categories.

  • Comparison of Means/Medians: Observe if the average exam scores are different for students who used support services compared to those who did not.
  • Comparison of Standard Deviations: Determine if the variability (consistency) in exam scores is different between the two groups. A larger standard deviation suggests more diverse scores within that group.
  • Comparison of Skewness/Kurtosis: Analyze if the shapes of the exam score distributions differ between the 'Yes' and 'No' groups (e.g., is one group's scores more skewed or more peaked?).
  • Box Plots: Visually compare the median lines, the size of the boxes (IQR), and the presence or absence of outliers between the groups. This provides a quick visual summary of potential group differences.
