Paired-Samples T-Test Using jamovi

A Guide to Understanding and Performing a Paired-Samples T-Test with jamovi

The paired-samples t-test is an example of a repeated measures or within-subjects design. A within-subjects design is when you are looking for differences in individuals across a series of measurements. In the guides for the independent-samples t-test and one-way ANOVA you were looking at between-subjects design, where individuals belonged to a particular group and differences were being examined between these groups. When there are two measurements per individual, for example a pre and post score, then we can make use of the paired-samples t-test.

Some examples of within-subject designs include:

  • For 2 repeated measures:
    • Measuring participants' anxiety levels before and after a mindfulness intervention.
    • Comparing a patient's blood pressure readings before and after administering a new medication.
    • Assessing a student's test score on a pre-test versus a post-test for the same course.
  • For 2+ repeated measures (leading to Repeated Measures ANOVA):
    • Tracking participants' reaction times across three different task conditions (e.g., low, medium, high cognitive load).
    • Measuring plant growth at weekly intervals over a month.
    • Evaluating the effectiveness of different therapeutic techniques on depression scores measured at baseline, mid-treatment, and post-treatment.

Assumptions of the Paired-Samples T-Test

For a paired-samples t-test, there are several key assumptions that should ideally be met for the results to be reliable. It's important to check these before interpreting your jamovi output:

  1. Dependent Variable: The dependent variable should be measured on a continuous scale (i.e., interval or ratio level). Examples include scores on a test, reaction time in milliseconds, or temperature in degrees Celsius.
  2. Independence of Observations: The observations within each pair are dependent (e.g., pre-score and post-score from the same person), but the pairs themselves must be independent of each other. This means that the data from one participant (or pair) should not influence the data from another participant (or pair). This is usually ensured by the study design.
  3. Normality of the Differences: The distribution of the differences between the two related groups should be approximately normally distributed. This is the most crucial assumption for the paired-samples t-test.
    • How to check: In jamovi, you can assess normality of the differences by:
      • Visually inspecting a histogram: Look for a roughly bell-shaped curve.
      • Examining Q-Q plots: Check if data points fall close to the diagonal line.
      • Using the Shapiro-Wilk test: A non-significant p-value (typically p > 0.05) indicates that the assumption of normality is met.
      • Calculating z-scores for skewness and kurtosis: Divide the skewness/kurtosis value by its standard error. Absolute z-scores within approximately ±1.96 (for a 0.05 significance level) suggest normality.

Does a training course improve students’ performance on a quiz?

In this guide you will be making use of the `quiz_score_pre` and `quiz_score_post` variables. The students were marked on a quiz score before starting the course and then again after the course. As the researcher you are interested in whether the course has made a statistically significant difference (p < 0.05) to their scores.

Null and Alternative Hypotheses of the Paired-Samples T-Test

The null hypothesis (H0) of a paired-samples t-test states that there is no statistically significant difference between the means of the two related groups (or that the mean difference is zero).

H0: μpre = μpost or μdifference = 0

The alternative hypothesis (H1) states that there is a statistically significant difference between the means of the two related groups (or that the mean difference is not zero).

H1: μpre ≠ μpost or μdifference ≠ 0

The t-test is carried out and produces a t-statistic and a p-value. If this p-value is less than a defined amount, normally 0.05 is used, then this gives evidence of there being a difference between the groups. If this is not the case then we have not found enough evidence that the groups make a difference to the score.

Normality Assumption Check

As part of the jamovi paired-samples t-test process, the software can perform the normality test using the Shapiro-Wilk test. If you prefer to use descriptive statistics and skew etc. you will first need to create the difference variable. This next part will lead you through creating the difference variable and then you can use knowledge gained from previous guides to check the normality of this variable.

Procedure to create the difference variable in jamovi:

  1. From the data, scroll to the right until you reach the first empty column.
  2. Double-click in the header of the empty column.
  3. From the options that appear at the top, choose New Computed Variable.
  4. In the first text box, enter a name for the variable, e.g., `Quiz Difference`.
  5. Click the button marked fx.
  6. From the Variables list, find the `quiz_score_post` variable and double-click it. It should appear in the function box.
  7. Now type a subtract sign (-) and double-click the `quiz_score_pre` variable. The formula should read `quiz_score_post - quiz_score_pre`.
  8. Press the up arrow to close the computed variable menu. You will now see the `Quiz Difference` variable in your data.

Descriptive Statistics for the Difference Variable:

To obtain descriptive statistics and normality tests for this new difference variable:

  1. Go to the Analysis tab in the ribbon.
  2. Click on Exploration > Descriptives.
  3. Move your newly created `Quiz Difference` variable from the left box to the "Variables" box on the right.
  4. Under "Statistics", ensure "Mean", "Median", "Standard deviation", "Minimum", "Maximum", "Skewness", "Std. error skewness", "Kurtosis", and "Std. error kurtosis" are checked.
  5. Under "Plots", check "Histogram" and "Q-Q plot".
  6. Under "Assumption Checks", check "Normality" (for the Shapiro-Wilk test).

Paired-Samples T-Test Procedure

To perform the paired-samples t-test in jamovi:

  1. Open your dataset in jamovi.
  2. Go to the Analysis tab in the ribbon.
  3. Click on T-Tests > Paired Samples T-Test.
  4. In the "Paired Samples T-Test" analysis panel, move both `quiz_score_pre` and `quiz_score_post` into the "Paired Variables" box. You can drag them individually or select them and use the arrow button.
  5. Under "Tests", ensure "Student's" is selected (this is the standard t-test).
  6. Under "Hypothesis", ensure "Measure 1 - Measure 2 ≠ 0" is selected. This option represents a two-tailed test, which is typically chosen when you are interested in detecting a difference between the two measures, but you don't have a specific prediction about the direction of that difference (i.e., whether scores will increase or decrease). It tests if the mean difference is significantly different from zero in either a positive or negative direction.
  7. Under "Additional Statistics", check the following options:
    • "Mean difference"
    • "Confidence interval" (this will provide the 95% confidence interval for the mean difference)
    • "Effect size" (this will provide Cohen's d)
    • "Descriptives"
  8. Under "Assumption Checks", check "Normality" (this will run the Shapiro-Wilk test on the difference scores).
  9. Under "Plots", check "Descriptive plots".

The jamovi output will automatically update as you select these options, displaying the results in the output panel.

Interpretation of Results

Paired Samples T-Test Table

The primary output for the paired-samples t-test will be similar to this:

statistic df p Mean difference SE difference Effect Size
quiz_score_pre quiz_score_post Student's t -13.9 99.0 <.001 -5.03 0.361 Cohen's d -1.39

Note. H a μ Measure 1 - Measure 2 ≠ 0

Here's what these values mean:

  • statistic (Student's t = -13.9): This is the calculated t-value for the paired-samples t-test. The negative sign indicates that the mean of the second variable (`quiz_score_post`) is higher than the first variable (`quiz_score_pre`), as jamovi calculates the mean difference as (Variable 1 - Variable 2).
  • df (99.0): These are the degrees of freedom, calculated as N - 1 (where N is the number of pairs). In this case, 100 - 1 = 99.
  • p (<.001): This is the p-value. Since p < 0.001, which is less than our conventional significance level of 0.05, we reject the null hypothesis. This indicates that there is a statistically significant difference between the pre-course and post-course quiz scores.
  • Mean difference (-5.03): This is the average difference between `quiz_score_pre` and `quiz_score_post`. A negative value here means that `quiz_score_post` was, on average, higher than `quiz_score_pre`. This aligns with our earlier calculation of `quiz_score_post - quiz_score_pre` being positive when we manually created the `Quiz Difference` variable.
  • SE difference (0.361): This is the standard error of the mean difference.
  • Effect Size (Cohen's d = -1.39): This is a measure of the magnitude of the observed effect.

Explanation of Cohen's d:

Cohen's d is a standardized measure of effect size that quantifies the difference between two means in standard deviation units. It tells you how large the difference is, independent of sample size. A larger absolute value of Cohen's d indicates a stronger effect.

  • Interpretation Guidelines (Cohen, 1988):
    • Small effect: d = 0.2
    • Medium effect: d = 0.5
    • Large effect: d = 0.8

In this case, Cohen's d = -1.39. The negative sign simply reflects the direction of the difference (post-score was higher than pre-score). An absolute value of 1.39 indicates a very large effect size, meaning the training course had a substantial impact on students' quiz scores.

Write-up Results

Example write-up of this analysis:

A paired-samples t-test was conducted to evaluate whether a training course improved students' performance on a quiz. The assumption of normality for the difference scores was assessed using the Shapiro-Wilk test, which was non-significant (W = 0.987, p = 0.447), indicating that the assumption was met. Descriptive statistics showed that the mean quiz score before the course was 66.0 (SD = 9.36), and after the course, it increased to 71.0 (SD = 9.69).

The paired-samples t-test revealed a statistically significant improvement in quiz scores after the training course, t(99) = -13.9, p < .001. The mean difference in scores was -5.03 (95% CI [-5.75, -4.31]), with post-course scores being higher. Cohen's d was -1.39, indicating a very large effect size, suggesting a substantial practical significance of the training course on quiz performance.
