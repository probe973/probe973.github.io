Assessing Normality using an AI Chatbot

A Guide to Checking Data Distribution for Statistical Analysis with AI

Important Warning about AI and Data Analysis:

While AI can be a powerful tool for generating insights and assisting with data analysis, it's crucial to understand its limitations. AI models can make mistakes, "hallucinate" data, or provide incorrect interpretations, especially if the input is unclear, ambiguous, or if it lacks real-world context.

Always verify the output of an AI model against your raw data and your understanding of statistical principles. Treat AI as a helpful assistant, not as a replacement for critical thinking and human oversight in data analysis. The examples in this guide assume you will input real data into the AI entity.

This guide will walk you through the process of assessing whether your data is "normally distributed" by interacting with an AI chatbot. Understanding the distribution of your data, especially its normality, is a critical step in statistical analysis. Many common statistical tests (called parametric tests) assume that the data for the variables you are analyzing follow a normal (bell-shaped) distribution. If your data significantly deviates from normality, using these tests might lead to inaccurate conclusions.

If your data is not normally distributed, you might need to consider non-parametric tests (which do not assume normality) or transform your data to make it more normal. Checking normality helps you decide the most appropriate statistical test for your research questions. This guide describes how to use prompts to get normality assessments from an AI chatbot.

Before you begin, ensure you have your data from the omni.csv file ready. You will typically upload this file to the AI chatbot. Throughout this guide, we will use specific variables from your dataset to illustrate the concepts:

  • final_exam_score: Represents the numerical score achieved by students on a final exam.
  • study_hours_per_week: Indicates the number of hours a student reported studying each week.
  • anxiety_score_pre: Anxiety level before a study intervention.
  • used_study_support_services: A categorical variable indicating whether a student utilized study support services (Yes/No). This variable will be used to demonstrate assessing normality for groups.

Part 1: Assessing Normality for a Single Interval/Ratio Variable (Whole Sample)

We will start by examining the normality of final_exam_score for the entire dataset using various methods.

1.1 Using Summary Statistics: Skewness and Kurtosis (and their Z-scores)

Skewness and Kurtosis are numerical measures that describe the shape of your data's distribution. They can provide initial hints about normality. You can ask the AI to calculate these for you.

  1. Provide a prompt to the AI chatbot asking for descriptive statistics, specifically including skewness and kurtosis. 
    
For the 'final_exam_score' variable, please provide the mean, median, skewness, kurtosis, and their standard errors. Also, calculate and include the Z-scores for skewness and kurtosis.
  2. The AI chatbot will provide descriptive statistics.

Example AI Output (excerpt for final_exam_score):


Variable: final_exam_score
N: 100
Mean: 74.71
Median: 75.00
Skewness: 0.003
Standard Error of Skewness: 0.240
Z-score for Skewness: 0.01
Kurtosis: -0.131
Standard Error of Kurtosis: 0.478
Z-score for Kurtosis: -0.27
Minimum: 60
Maximum: 89

Interpretation:

  • Mean & Median Relationship: For a perfectly symmetrical distribution (like a normal distribution), the Mean and Median values will be very similar or identical.
    • If the Mean is greater than the Median, it suggests a positively skewed distribution (tail extends to the right).
    • If the Mean is less than the Median, it suggests a negatively skewed distribution (tail extends to the left).
  • Skewness: A value of 0 indicates perfect symmetry. Positive values mean a tail to the right (data clustered on the left), and negative values mean a tail to the left (data clustered on the right). For a normal distribution, skewness should be close to 0.
  • Kurtosis: A value of 0 indicates peakedness similar to a normal distribution. Positive values (leptokurtic) mean a more peaked distribution with fatter tails (more outliers), while negative values (platykurtic) mean a flatter distribution with thinner tails. For a normal distribution, kurtosis should be close to 0.
  • Standardised Skewness/Kurtosis (Z-scores): These Z-scores, provided directly by the AI, formally assess if skewness and kurtosis deviate significantly from normal. The formulas used are:
    • The Z-score for skewness is calculated as: 
      Z_skew = Skewness / Standard Error of Skewness
    • The Z-score for kurtosis is calculated as: 
      Z_kurt = Kurtosis / Standard Error of Kurtosis
    • If the absolute value of the Z-score for both skewness and kurtosis is less than 1.96 (i.e., between -1.96 and +1.96), the distribution is considered sufficiently normal for most practical purposes at a p < 0.05 significance level. Values outside this range suggest significant deviation from normality.

1.2 Visual Assessment with Histograms

A histogram allows you to visually inspect the shape of your data's distribution. A truly normal distribution will look like a symmetric bell curve. You can ask the AI to generate a description of this plot.

  1. Provide a prompt to the AI chatbot asking for a histogram. 
    
Describe the histogram for the 'final_exam_score' variable, including its shape and whether a normal distribution curve would fit it well.
  2. The AI chatbot should provide a textual description of the histogram. For a visual representation, you might need to use dedicated statistical software. You can ask the AI to provide code for this if needed (e.g., "Provide R code to generate this histogram").

Visual Interpretation: Look for symmetry, a single peak in the middle, and tails that taper off evenly on both sides. Compare the described bars to an imagined normal curve.

1.3 Visual Assessment with Q-Q Plots (Quantile-Quantile Plots)

Q-Q plots are a powerful visual tool for assessing normality. They plot the quantiles of your data against the quantiles of a theoretical normal distribution. If your data is normally distributed, the points will fall approximately along a straight diagonal line. Ask the AI for a description of this plot.

  1. Provide a prompt to the AI chatbot asking for a Q-Q plot description. 
    
Describe the Q-Q plot for 'final_exam_score'.
  2. The AI chatbot should provide a textual description of the Q-Q plot. Similar to histograms, for a visual representation, you might need to use dedicated statistical software and can ask the AI for code.

Visual Interpretation:

  • If points generally follow the diagonal line, the data is likely normal.
  • Deviations from the line, especially at the ends, indicate non-normality (e.g., S-shapes for skewness, tails curving away for kurtosis).

1.4 Statistical Test for Normality: Shapiro-Wilk Test

While visual inspection is important, statistical tests provide a numerical assessment of normality. The Shapiro-Wilk test is widely used for this purpose. You can ask the AI to perform this test.

  1. Provide a prompt to the AI chatbot asking for the Shapiro-Wilk test results. 
    
Perform a Shapiro-Wilk normality test for 'final_exam_score' and provide the p-value.
  2. The AI chatbot will provide the test results, including the p-value.

Interpretation of Shapiro-Wilk Test:

  • Null Hypothesis (H0): The data is normally distributed.
  • Alternative Hypothesis (H1): The data is NOT normally distributed.
  • If the p-value is greater than or equal to your significance level (e.g., p ≥ 0.05), you do not reject the null hypothesis. This suggests there is not enough evidence to conclude that the data is non-normal, so it can be considered normal for practical purposes.
  • If the p-value is less than your significance level (e.g., p < 0.05), you reject the null hypothesis. This suggests there is evidence that the data is NOT normally distributed.
  • Important Note: For very large sample sizes, the Shapiro-Wilk test can show a statistically significant deviation from normality even for minor, practically irrelevant deviations. Always combine the statistical test results with textual descriptions of visual inspections and also consider the standardised skewness and kurtosis. For small samples, the test might not be powerful enough to detect non-normality.

Part 2: Assessing Normality for an Interval/Ratio Variable (Split by Groups)

Sometimes, a variable might appear normally distributed in the overall sample, but its distribution might differ when examined within specific subgroups. This is important to check if you plan to compare groups using parametric tests.

We will assess the normality of final_exam_score again, but this time split by used_study_support_services (Yes/No).

Conclusion

You have now explored key methods for assessing the normality of your data using an AI chatbot: obtaining numerical summaries (Mean, Median, Skewness, Kurtosis, and their standardised Z-scores), requesting textual descriptions of visual tools (Histograms and Q-Q plots), and performing statistical tests (Shapiro-Wilk). You've also learned how to ask the AI to apply these assessments to your entire sample and to specific subgroups within your data.

Remember that no single method provides a definitive answer to normality. Always use a combination of numerical and statistical results, combined with textual descriptions of visual inspections, and the standardised skewness and kurtosis Z-scores. Also, consider your sample size. This thorough approach will help you make informed decisions about whether to proceed with parametric tests or consider alternative non-parametric methods for your analyses.

Using AI chatbots can significantly streamline your data analysis workflow, providing quick insights and textual summaries of data characteristics based on simple, natural language prompts. Continue experimenting with different variables and data structures to become more proficient in leveraging AI for your statistical needs.
