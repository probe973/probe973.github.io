Assessing Normality in SPSS

A Guide to Checking Data Distribution for Statistical Analysis

This guide will walk you through the process of assessing whether your data is "normally distributed" using SPSS. Understanding the distribution of your data, especially its normality, is a critical step in statistical analysis. Many common statistical tests (called parametric tests) assume that the data for the variables you are analyzing follow a normal (bell-shaped) distribution. If your data significantly deviates from normality, using these tests might lead to inaccurate conclusions.

If your data is not normally distributed, you might need to consider non-parametric tests (which do not assume normality) or transform your data to make it more normal. Checking normality helps you decide the most appropriate statistical test for your research questions. This guide describes analysis within SPSS and assumes your data is already correctly set up in an SPSS `.sav` file.

Before you begin, ensure you have your omni.sav file open in SPSS. Throughout this guide, we will use specific variables from your dataset to illustrate the concepts:

  • final_exam_score: Represents the numerical score achieved by students on a final exam.
  • study_hours_per_week: Indicates the number of hours a student reported studying each week.
  • anxiety_score_pre: Anxiety level before a study intervention.
  • used_study_support_services: A categorical variable indicating whether a student utilized study support services (Yes/No). This variable will be used to demonstrate assessing normality for groups.

Part 1: Assessing Normality for a Single Interval/Ratio Variable (Whole Sample)

We will start by examining the normality of final_exam_score for the entire dataset using various methods.

1.1 Using Summary Statistics: Skewness and Kurtosis (and their Z-scores)

Skewness and Kurtosis are numerical measures that describe the shape of your data's distribution. They can provide initial hints about normality. SPSS provides these values directly, along with their standard errors.

  1. In the SPSS menu, click on Analyze > Descriptive Statistics > Frequencies...
  2. A new dialog box will appear. On the left, find final_exam_score and move it to the Variables(s): box on the right using the arrow button.
  3. In the `Frequencies` dialog box, click the Statistics... button.

    Under Distribution, select: Skewness, Kurtosis. (These will automatically provide their standard errors in the output).

    Click Continue, and ensure `Display frequency tables` is unchecked (as these are generally not useful for continuous variables). Then click `OK` in the main Frequencies dialog box. SPSS will generate the output.

  4. Alternatively, you can use the more comprehensive `Explore` function: Go to Analyze > Descriptive Statistics > Explore... Move final_exam_score to the Dependent List: box. Click on the Statistics... button and ensure Descriptives is checked. Click Continue, then OK. This will also provide Skewness and Kurtosis along with their standard errors.

In this output, Skewness is the skewness value and Kurtosis is the kurtosis value. Note that SPSS, by default, reports **excess kurtosis**, where a value of 0 indicates a normal distribution.

Interpretation:

  • Skewness: A value of 0 indicates perfect symmetry. Positive values mean a tail to the right (data clustered on the left), and negative values mean a tail to the left (data clustered on the right). For a normal distribution, skewness should be close to 0.
  • Kurtosis: A value of 0 indicates peakedness similar to a normal distribution (mesokurtic). Positive values (leptokurtic) mean a more peaked distribution with fatter tails (more outliers), while negative values (platykurtic) mean a flatter distribution with thinner tails. For a normal distribution, excess kurtosis should be close to 0.
  • Standardised Skewness/Kurtosis (Z-scores): To formally assess if skewness and kurtosis deviate significantly from normal, you can convert them to Z-scores. SPSS provides their standard errors directly in the output.
    • The Z-score for skewness is calculated as: 
      Z_skew = Skewness / Std. Error of Skewness
    • The Z-score for kurtosis is calculated as: 
      Z_kurt = Kurtosis / Std. Error of Kurtosis
      (This formula is used because SPSS reports 'excess kurtosis', where 0 is the expected value for a normal distribution).
    • If the absolute value of the Z-score for both skewness and kurtosis is less than 1.96 (i.e., between -1.96 and +1.96), the distribution is considered sufficiently normal for most practical purposes at a p < 0.05 significance level. Values outside this range suggest significant deviation from normality.

1.2 Visual Assessment with Histograms

A histogram allows you to visually inspect the shape of your data's distribution. A truly normal distribution will look like a symmetric bell curve.

  1. Go to Analyze > Descriptive Statistics > Frequencies... (same as Section 1.1). Move final_exam_score to the Variables(s): box.
  2. Click on the Charts... button. Select Histograms and check Show normal curve on histogram. Click Continue, then OK.
  3. Alternatively, use `Explore`: Go to Analyze > Descriptive Statistics > Explore..., move final_exam_score to Dependent List:, click Plots... and check Histogram.

1.3 Visual Assessment with Q-Q Plots (Quantile-Quantile Plots)

Q-Q plots are a powerful visual tool for assessing normality. They plot the quantiles of your data against the quantiles of a theoretical normal distribution. If your data is normally distributed, the points will fall approximately along a straight diagonal line.

  1. Go to Analyze > Descriptive Statistics > Explore... (This is recommended as it also includes the Shapiro-Wilk test). Move final_exam_score to the Dependent List: box. Click on the Plots... button. Check Normality plots with tests. Click Continue, then OK.
  2. Alternatively, for just the Q-Q plot: Go to Analyze > Descriptive Statistics > Q-Q Plots..., move final_exam_score to the Variables: box, and click OK.

Visual Interpretation:

  • If points generally follow the diagonal line, the data is likely normal.
  • Deviations from the line, especially at the ends, indicate non-normality (e.g., S-shapes for skewness, tails curving away for kurtosis).

1.4 Statistical Test for Normality: Shapiro-Wilk Test

While visual inspection is important, statistical tests provide a numerical assessment of normality. The Shapiro-Wilk test is widely used for this purpose.

  1. Go to Analyze > Descriptive Statistics > Explore... Move final_exam_score to the Dependent List: box. Click on the Plots... button. Check Normality plots with tests. Click Continue, then OK.
  2. The Shapiro-Wilk test results will be found in the "Tests of Normality" table within the SPSS Output Viewer.

Interpretation of Shapiro-Wilk Test:

  • Null Hypothesis (H0): The data is normally distributed.
  • Alternative Hypothesis (H1): The data is NOT normally distributed.
  • If the p-value (Sig.) is greater than or equal to your significance level (e.g., p ≥ 0.05), you do not reject the null hypothesis. This suggests there is not enough evidence to conclude that the data is non-normal, so it can be considered normal for practical purposes.
  • If the p-value (Sig.) is less than your significance level (e.g., p < 0.05), you reject the null hypothesis. This suggests there is evidence that the data is NOT normally distributed.
  • Important Note: For very large sample sizes, the Shapiro-Wilk test can show a statistically significant deviation from normality even for minor, practically irrelevant deviations. Always combine the statistical test results with visual inspections (histograms, Q-Q plots) and also consider the Z-scores for skewness and kurtosis. For small samples, the test might not be powerful enough to detect non-normality.

Part 2: Assessing Normality for an Interval/Ratio Variable (Split by Groups)

Sometimes, a variable might appear normally distributed in the overall sample, but its distribution might differ when examined within specific subgroups. This is important to check if you plan to compare groups using parametric tests.

We will assess the normality of final_exam_score again, but this time split by used_study_support_services (Yes/No).

2.1 Grouped Summary Statistics and Plots

SPSS's `Explore` function is ideal for generating grouped descriptive statistics and normality plots/tests.

  1. In the SPSS menu, click on Analyze > Descriptive Statistics > Explore...
  2. Move final_exam_score to the Dependent List: box.
  3. Now, find the categorical variable used_study_support_services and move it to the Factor List: box.
  4. Click on the Statistics... button and ensure Descriptives is checked. Click Continue.
  5. Click on the Plots... button. Check Histogram and Normality plots with tests. Uncheck Stem-and-leaf if you don't need it. Click Continue.
  6. Click OK in the main `Explore` dialog box. SPSS will generate separate descriptive statistics tables, histograms, Q-Q plots, and normality tests for each category of used_study_support_services.

2.2 Interpreting Grouped Normality Assessments

Examine the output for each group independently, just as you did in Part 1. Pay close attention to:

  • Skewness and Kurtosis values: Are they close to zero for each group?
  • Calculated Z-scores for Skewness and Kurtosis: Based on the Skewness/Kurtosis values and their standard errors for each group, calculate the Z-scores. Do they suggest significant deviation from normality (e.g., |Z| > 1.96)?
  • Histograms: Do they show a bell shape for each group? Are there differences in symmetry or tails between groups?
  • Q-Q Plots: Do the points follow the line for each group? Are there deviations, especially at the tails?
  • Shapiro-Wilk p-values: For each group, is the p-value (Sig.) ≥ 0.05 (suggesting normality) or < 0.05 (suggesting non-normality)?

Conclusion

You have now explored key methods for assessing the normality of your data in SPSS: using numerical summaries (Skewness and Kurtosis, including their standardised Z-scores), visual tools (Histograms and Q-Q plots), and statistical tests (Shapiro-Wilk). You've also learned how to apply these assessments to your entire sample and to specific subgroups within your data.

Remember that no single method provides a definitive answer to normality. Always use a combination of visual inspection, statistical tests, and the standardised skewness and kurtosis Z-scores. Also, consider your sample size. This thorough approach will help you make informed decisions about whether to proceed with parametric tests or consider alternative non-parametric methods for your analyses.
